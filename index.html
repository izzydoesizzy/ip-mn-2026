Quick Reference

CSM & SaaS terms you'll hear.

Quick definitions so you can follow along and use the language naturally in conversations with Michael and the team.

ACV

Annual Contract Value — the yearly revenue per customer. For Mighty Pro, roughly $28–30K per Host annually.

Churn / Churn rate

% of customers who cancel or downgrade in a given period. For CSMs, lowering churn = job security.

Community Health / Health Score

A metric (usually composite) measuring whether a community is thriving. Often includes engagement, retention, growth, activity.

Expansion / Expansion revenue

Revenue from existing customers who upgrade, add seats, or increase usage. Often higher margin than new customer revenue.

NRR (Net Revenue Retention)

Whether a customer base is growing or shrinking. NRR > 100% means expansion outpaces churn. Good indicator of CS health.

Onboarding

The critical first 30–90 days helping a new Host get live, trained, and seeing value. Often determines renewal likelihood.

Playbook / SOP

A documented process. E.g., "onboarding playbook" = step-by-step for bringing a Host live. SOPs = Standard Operating Procedures.

Renewal

When a customer's contract ends and they re-sign (or don't). Renewals are the baseline CSM metric. Losing a renewal = churn.

Time to value (TTV)

How long it takes a new customer to see tangible benefit. Shorter = higher retention. For Mighty, getting a Host to first member signup fast.

Upsell / Cross-sell

Upsell = larger plan or tier. Cross-sell = different product/feature. Both = expansion revenue. CSMs often own these conversations.

Account Health / Risk flagging

Watching for warning signs (declining engagement, support tickets, inactivity). Catching at-risk accounts early prevents churn.

QBR (Quarterly Business Review)

Formal check-in with a customer to review performance, align on goals, and plan the next quarter. Often with executives.

Be Ready to Get Pushed Live

The roleplay frameworks.

You already did written scenarios for the assignment. Michael will very likely push you into the live version — he plays the Host, you play the CSM. Expect variants of the two you wrote plus one fresh one.

Your five moves — use these on any roleplay

Ask a clarifying question before launching in. “Before I respond, can I ask what's driving this for you?” Shows diagnostic instinct.
Name the emotion you're hearing. “It sounds like you're frustrated, and honestly I'd be frustrated too.” Don't skip this step.
Don't rush to a solution. Rushing reads as defensive. Sit in the problem for a beat.
Always end with a concrete next step. “Here's what I'll do by Friday” >>> “let me circle back.”
If you don't know, say so. “I don't have that answer, but here's how I'd find out within 24 hours” is a correct answer.
Scenario 01 · Variant of assignment scenario

The dependent, inherited Host

“You've just taken over a Host account from another CSM who left. The Host is calling you now. They're anxious about the handoff and their renewal is in 60 days.”

What he's testing: build trust fast, handle emotional transitions, reset expectations without damaging the relationship.

  • Open by acknowledging the transition openly. Don't pretend it's business-as-usual.
  • Ask what was working well before, and what they wish had been different.
  • Commit to a specific short-term action: “In the next week, I'll send you a fresh read on your account and my initial recommendations, then let's meet to align.”
  • Separate the renewal from the trust-building. Don't let the renewal timeline pressure the first conversation.
Scenario 02 · Variant of assignment scenario

The delayed app launch + refund request

“A Host is on the phone. Their Mighty Pro app launch has been delayed three weeks past the date we gave them. They want a refund. Go.”

What he's testing: de-escalation, ownership, creative problem-solving under commercial pressure.

  • Own the delay. No excuses, no blaming other teams. “We missed the date we committed to. That's on us.”
  • Acknowledge the real cost to them — launch marketing, audience expectations, revenue timing.
  • Ask what they need to feel made whole. Sometimes it's not a refund: an extension, a credit, a white-glove launch, or an exec escalation they can see.
  • Be clear what you can commit to in the call, and commit to a decision timeline for the rest. “I'll come back to you with an answer by end of day tomorrow.”
  • Protect the long-term relationship even if a partial refund happens. The goal is that they stay and become advocates.
Scenario 03 · Likely fresh one

Renewal at risk

“A Host tells you they're not sure they're going to renew. They're not getting the engagement they expected. What do you say?”

  • Don't defend. Ask questions first: what did they expect, what are they measuring, what would success have looked like by now.
  • Diagnose together: product issue, strategy issue, execution issue on their side, or misaligned expectation from the start? Each has a different fix.
  • Bring data. Mighty's benchmarks (59% weekly active return, 80%+ revenue retention, 84% member-created content) give reference points.
  • Offer a concrete re-engagement plan with a defined timeline before the renewal date. “30-day plan — let's attack three specific levers and regroup in four weeks.”
  • If the fit is genuinely wrong, say so. Trying to save a bad fit costs both sides more long-term.
