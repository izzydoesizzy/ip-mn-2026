What attracted you to Mighty? Why this CSM role specifically?

Three specifics, not a list of features:

  1. Mighty Pro's Host roster — serious operators, not casual creators. The work matters.
  2. Community Design™ as a real framework, not marketing copy. CSM is consultative.
  3. The opportunity to bring AI fluency into the Host workflow at scale.
B · CS as a P&L function
Walk me through how you'd approach a $30K Host's renewal at 6 months out. 3 months. 1 month.
  • 6 months: value audit. ROI to date, where they're stuck, what the next-year case looks like. Stakeholder map updated.
  • 3 months: alignment meeting with the decision-maker. Success plan reviewed. Expansion conversation if signals are right.
  • 1 month: no surprises. Renewal terms aligned in writing. This is execution, not negotiation.

Add: red flags — engagement drops, champion change, competitive chatter — never wait for the renewal window. Address the moment they surface.

How would you spot an at-risk account before it's obvious?

Leading over lagging. Specific signals:

  • Champion goes quiet or changes role.
  • Weekly active members, member-created content, event attendance trending down.
  • Host stops investing on their side — no new content, no programming.
  • Conversations shift from “how do I grow this” to “how do I justify this.”
  • Reschedules. Skipped QBRs. Silence.
How do you think about expansion vs. retention?

Retention is the floor; expansion is the ceiling. The CSM owns both, but expansion only happens when retention is real — you can't upsell a Host who isn't getting value. Lead with delivering the win, then surface the next problem the Host doesn't know they have. NRR > GRR is the scoreboard. If she pushes: cite a specific Dutchie or ClearCareer instance where you saw the upgrade signal early and acted on it.

How do you build a success plan for a brand-new Host? What goes in it?

Start with their goals, not your template. What does success look like in 12 months — member count, engagement, revenue, retention, content velocity? Who are the stakeholders, what does each care about? Then work backwards: 30/60/90, then quarterly. Build it with the Host, not for them, so they own it. The plan is a living doc, not a deliverable.

C · Coaching philosophy
Walk me through how you'd handle a Host who is struggling but defensive.

This is her test for whether you can coach. Don't problem-solve at her. Walk through it:

  1. First call: stay curious, don't diagnose. Ask “what does success look like for you here” before “here's what I'd change.”
  2. Name what you're seeing without judgement. (“The data shows X. What's your read?”)
  3. Hand them ownership of the next move, not a prescription.
  4. Schedule a check-in. Don't disappear.

If you can quote her own dialect — “what do you want to accomplish” — do it naturally, not as performance.

How do you balance giving advice vs. asking questions?

Advice when the stakes are technical or time-sensitive. Questions when the work is theirs to own. Most of the time, asking is faster than telling because they don't push back on a decision they made. The exception: if the Host is stuck and asking for a recommendation, give them one — coaching theatre when they need a guide is its own failure mode.

Tell me about a hard conversation that became the win.

Pick a Dutchie or ClearCareer story where the hard conversation itself was the value. Not where you avoided it gracefully. Structure: situation → what most CSMs would have done → what you did instead → outcome → the lesson about reading clients. End with a number.

D · Founder → IC self-awareness
What will be hard about going from founder to IC on someone else's team?

Honest answer: not seeing the full strategic picture some days. Trusting decisions you didn't make. Resisting the urge to optimize beyond your scope.

Counter-balance: this is precisely what you're signing up for — the leverage of a real platform, the rhythm of working alongside teammates, the discipline of doing CS exceptionally vs. being the everywhere person.

What's an area you're actively working on?

Pick something real, not disqualifying. Avoid clichés. Strong options:

  • Pushing back with executive stakeholders more directly — founder reflex is to absorb, not redirect.
  • Sharper commercial instincts on quoting and renewals.
  • Going deeper on a single domain rather than broad surface area.
What kind of manager gets the best work out of you?

Someone who gives context and trust. Direct feedback, not hand-holding — but available when you're stuck or when an escalation needs backing. Mary will hear this as “you understand what a good manager-IC contract looks like.” She knows; she's run a team.

Where do you see yourself in 2–3 years?

The CSM others come to for the hardest accounts, on a path toward shaping CS at a portfolio level. Don't promise you want her job. Don't say “I just want to keep learning” — reads as unambitious. Calibrated, hungry, specific.

Quick Reference

CSM & SaaS terms you'll hear.

Quick definitions so you can follow along and use the language naturally in conversations with Michael and the team.

ACV

Annual Contract Value — the yearly revenue per customer. For Mighty Pro, roughly $28–30K per Host annually.

Churn / Churn rate

% of customers who cancel or downgrade in a given period. For CSMs, lowering churn = job security.

Community Health / Health Score

A metric (usually composite) measuring whether a community is thriving. Often includes engagement, retention, growth, activity.

Expansion / Expansion revenue

Revenue from existing customers who upgrade, add seats, or increase usage. Often higher margin than new customer revenue.

NRR (Net Revenue Retention)

Whether a customer base is growing or shrinking. NRR > 100% means expansion outpaces churn. Good indicator of CS health.

Onboarding

The critical first 30–90 days helping a new Host get live, trained, and seeing value. Often determines renewal likelihood.

Playbook / SOP

A documented process. E.g., "onboarding playbook" = step-by-step for bringing a Host live. SOPs = Standard Operating Procedures.

Renewal

When a customer's contract ends and they re-sign (or don't). Renewals are the baseline CSM metric. Losing a renewal = churn.

Time to value (TTV)

How long it takes a new customer to see tangible benefit. Shorter = higher retention. For Mighty, getting a Host to first member signup fast.

Upsell / Cross-sell

Upsell = larger plan or tier. Cross-sell = different product/feature. Both = expansion revenue. CSMs often own these conversations.

Account Health / Risk flagging

Watching for warning signs (declining engagement, support tickets, inactivity). Catching at-risk accounts early prevents churn.

QBR (Quarterly Business Review)

Formal check-in with a customer to review performance, align on goals, and plan the next quarter. Often with executives.
