Tell me about a Host transformation you'd be proud to share.

Use the language she uses. Not “client.” Host transformation. Pick a ClearCareer client where you held the line on what they actually needed (not what they were asking for) and they got there. Lead with the client's outcome, not your tactic. Land a number.

What attracted you to Mighty? Why this CSM role specifically?

Three specifics, not a list of features:

  1. Mighty Pro's Host roster — serious operators, not casual creators. The work matters.
  2. Community Design™ as a real framework, not marketing copy. CSM is consultative.
  3. The opportunity to bring AI fluency into the Host workflow at scale.
B · CS as a P&L function
Walk me through how you'd approach a $30K Host's renewal at 6 months out. 3 months. 1 month.
  • 6 months: value audit. ROI to date, where they're stuck, what the next-year case looks like. Stakeholder map updated.
  • 3 months: alignment meeting with the decision-maker. Success plan reviewed. Expansion conversation if signals are right.
  • 1 month: no surprises. Renewal terms aligned in writing. This is execution, not negotiation.

Add: red flags — engagement drops, champion change, competitive chatter — never wait for the renewal window. Address the moment they surface.

How would you spot an at-risk account before it's obvious?

Leading over lagging. Specific signals:

  • Champion goes quiet or changes role.
  • Weekly active members, member-created content, event attendance trending down.
  • Host stops investing on their side — no new content, no programming.
  • Conversations shift from “how do I grow this” to “how do I justify this.”
  • Reschedules. Skipped QBRs. Silence.
How do you think about expansion vs. retention?

Retention is the floor; expansion is the ceiling. The CSM owns both, but expansion only happens when retention is real — you can't upsell a Host who isn't getting value. Lead with delivering the win, then surface the next problem the Host doesn't know they have. NRR > GRR is the scoreboard. If she pushes: cite a specific Dutchie or ClearCareer instance where you saw the upgrade signal early and acted on it.

How do you build a success plan for a brand-new Host? What goes in it?

Start with their goals, not your template. What does success look like in 12 months — member count, engagement, revenue, retention, content velocity? Who are the stakeholders, what does each care about? Then work backwards: 30/60/90, then quarterly. Build it with the Host, not for them, so they own it. The plan is a living doc, not a deliverable.

C · Coaching philosophy
Walk me through how you'd handle a Host who is struggling but defensive.

This is her test for whether you can coach. Don't problem-solve at her. Walk through it:

  1. First call: stay curious, don't diagnose. Ask “what does success look like for you here” before “here's what I'd change.”
  2. Name what you're seeing without judgement. (“The data shows X. What's your read?”)
  3. Hand them ownership of the next move, not a prescription.
  4. Schedule a check-in. Don't disappear.

If you can quote her own dialect — “what do you want to accomplish” — do it naturally, not as performance.

How do you balance giving advice vs. asking questions?

Advice when the stakes are technical or time-sensitive. Questions when the work is theirs to own. Most of the time, asking is faster than telling because they don't push back on a decision they made. The exception: if the Host is stuck and asking for a recommendation, give them one — coaching theatre when they need a guide is its own failure mode.

Tell me about a hard conversation that became the win.

Pick a Dutchie or ClearCareer story where the hard conversation itself was the value. Not where you avoided it gracefully. Structure: situation → what most CSMs would have done → what you did instead → outcome → the lesson about reading clients. End with a number.

D · Founder → IC self-awareness
What will be hard about going from founder to IC on someone else's team?

Honest answer: not seeing the full strategic picture some days. Trusting decisions you didn't make. Resisting the urge to optimize beyond your scope.

Counter-balance: this is precisely what you're signing up for — the leverage of a real platform, the rhythm of working alongside teammates, the discipline of doing CS exceptionally vs. being the everywhere person.

What's an area you're actively working on?

Pick something real, not disqualifying. Avoid clichés. Strong options:

  • Pushing back with executive stakeholders more directly — founder reflex is to absorb, not redirect.
  • Sharper commercial instincts on quoting and renewals.
  • Going deeper on a single domain rather than broad surface area.
What kind of manager gets the best work out of you?

Someone who gives context and trust. Direct feedback, not hand-holding — but available when you're stuck or when an escalation needs backing. Mary will hear this as “you understand what a good manager-IC contract looks like.” She knows; she's run a team.

Where do you see yourself in 2–3 years?

The CSM others come to for the hardest accounts, on a path toward shaping CS at a portfolio level. Don't promise you want her job. Don't say “I just want to keep learning” — reads as unambitious. Calibrated, hungry, specific.

If She Pushes You Live

Three Mary-flavored roleplays.

The generic frameworks on page 17 cover any interviewer. Mary will push you on Socratic posture, on the renewal motion in real time, and on whether founder muscle memory will get in the way. Rehearse these three out loud.

Mary Scenario 01 · Socratic posture probe

“Tell me about a moment you wanted to give the answer but didn't.”

She leans back. She is testing whether you can stay in inquiry when telling would feel safer. Likely follow-up: “What was the question you asked instead?”

Three moves:

Pick a story where the right answer was the client's, not yours. Alison or Tasha. Don't pick a story where you eventually told the answer.
Quote the actual question you asked. Not paraphrased. “If money and status weren't the constraint, what does the job feel like at the end of a good day?”
Land on: “their answer was sharper than mine would have been.” Acknowledge the trade you made — you were quieter; they got further.

Trap: don't pick a story where you eventually circled back and gave the answer. She wants to know you can hold the silence.

Mary Scenario 02 · Renewal at risk in real time

“A Host called my CSM Friday saying they're done. Walk me through Monday morning.”

She is testing diagnostic-first thinking under commercial pressure. The reflex she wants you to resist: jumping straight to a save plan.

Three moves:

Diagnose before you plan. “Before Monday I'd want to know what the Friday call surfaced. What did they say, and what did they mean? Where's the gap between expectation and what they're seeing?”
Reframe the win. “The win isn't ‘save the renewal.’ The win is: do they want to stay, do they want to go better than they came in, or is this genuinely a wrong fit. Each one has a different play.”
The 30-day plan. Three specific levers. Weekly check-ins. Owner on each side. Decision date before the renewal window. Bring Mighty's benchmarks if relevant: 59% weekly active, 80%+ revenue retention, 84% member-created content.

Trap: don't promise to save it on Monday. Promise to diagnose it cleanly by end-of-week and make a call.

Mary Scenario 03 · The founder objection

“I get a lot of founders pitching for IC seats. Why won't you flame out?”

She might not phrase it this directly. The probe will be there regardless — usually disguised as the founder-to-IC self-awareness question. Don't wait for it; surface it on your terms.

Three moves:

Acknowledge the pattern is real. “You should be skeptical. The pattern you're describing is real. Founders who treat IC seats as an interim fallback flame out, and they cost the team months.”
Mirror her own move. “You ran Marlow for seven years before Mighty as Director. Year one was learning the seat. You earned the VP after. That's the move I want to make — same arc, different chapter.”
Concrete commitment. “First 90 days I'm not optimizing your motion. I'm shadowing it, listening, learning what the team has earned. If I see something worth flagging at month twelve, it'll come through my manager as a question, not a manifesto.”

Trap: don't promise you'll never want a leadership role. Sounds dishonest and unambitious. Calibrated, hungry, specific.

Speak Her Language Without Faking Certification

Erickson dialect — four lines that land.

Mary is Erickson-certified. Erickson coaching is solution-focused, agenda-of-the-coachee, and built around the “powerful question.” You can drop these phrases naturally because you already work this way — you just learned it by doing, not by paying for the letters. Use sparingly. One or two per call is plenty; four is performance.

Term 01

“Powerful question.”

Erickson term for an open, evocative question that surfaces the coachee's clarity. No yes/no answer. Makes them stop and think.

Drop when: she asks about your coaching philosophy. “I think of those as powerful questions — the ones that make them stop and think, not the ones that get a quick answer.”

Term 02

“Coachee's agenda.”

Central Erickson distinction: the agenda belongs to the coachee, not the coach. Drift to the coach's agenda and the work degrades.

Drop when: she asks about advice vs. questions. “Most of my work is staying on the coachee's agenda. The moment I drift to my agenda, the work degrades.”

Term 03

“Solution-focused.”

Erickson is solution-focused, not analytic. Doesn't excavate root causes endlessly. Builds forward from current strengths.

Drop when: she asks how you handle a stuck Host. “I lean solution-focused. We don't litigate why they're stuck. We map what they want and where the next move is.”

Term 04

“Acknowledgment vs. praise.”

Erickson distinction. Acknowledgment is specific, evidence-based, named. Praise is generic, evaluative, hollow.

Drop when: she asks how you build trust with a struggling Host. “I work in acknowledgment, not praise. I name what I see, evidence-based. Praise reads hollow; acknowledgment lands.”

The honest disclaimer if she pushes

“I'm not certified Erickson. I read your published work because I wanted to understand the dialect on the team I'd be joining. The methods themselves — powerful questions, coachee's agenda, solution-focused — I learned doing the work, not by paying for the letters. The model you used to scale Marlow's coaches is the model I work in.”

Sequence Matters

Five questions, sequenced for the call.

Beyond the nine on page 08. These are sequenced for when in the call to deploy. Don't ask them all — pick the two or three that match the energy. The opener and closer are non-negotiable.

01 · Opener · her own words back to her
“Mary, in a 2022 interview about Marlow you said something that's stuck with me. You said you didn't start your business for balance — that the business was your passion, that you'd found your blend with a four-month-old at home. Two years later you joined Mighty as Director, then VP. What changed? What made working alongside someone else's platform the right move for you at that moment?”
Why this lands: quotes her own words back to her, shows you've read her deeply (not just the public bio), mirrors the founder → IC arc you're making yourself. Invites her to walk you through her move — the parallel you'll need her to see when she asks why you're leaving the founder seat. Don't rush. Let her sit with it. The Marlow-to-Mighty story is hers to tell, and she'll tell it well if you give her room.
02 · Mid-call · two-axis thinking
How do you measure success for a Host vs. for a CSM? Where do those metrics agree, and where do they pull in different directions?
Proves you think in two axes — exactly the founder-to-IC thinking she's testing for. Reveals whether the team has language for the tension or improvises it.
03 · Mid-call · the dialect bridge
Erickson is solution-focused. In a commercial CS context where retention dollars matter, what's the hardest tradeoff you've had to make between coaching purity and commercial pressure?
Shows you understand her dialect AND the commercial pressure she navigates. High-trust signal. Risk: only ask if you're ready for a five-minute answer; she'll give one.
04 · Late · invite her to coach you
You've made the founder-to-IC move yourself. What's the part of it that took you longer than you expected?
Disarms her concern that you don't know what you're walking into. Asks her to coach you on her own move — flattering without sucking up. Strong rapport-builder near the end of the call.
05 · Closer · surface objections
Is there anything about my background — founder pivot, the coaching dialect, retention specifics — where you'd want more detail or have a concern I can address before we wrap?
Same as the page-08 closer, sharpened with the three specific concerns from page 12. Naming them unprompted is itself the proof of self-awareness. Always ask this. No exceptions.

The pacing rule

Mary is a coach. She'll create silence. Don't fill it. After your 90-second intro, after each story, after each of these questions — pause. Two to three seconds of silence is a feature, not a bug. The pause is itself the proof you can hold inquiry.
